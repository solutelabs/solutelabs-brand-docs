## Who We Are

SoluteLabs is an AI-native product engineering studio. Founded in 2014. Headquartered in Ahmedabad, India, with a US entity in Delaware.

We deliver like a team 3–5x our size — because we rebuilt our entire development lifecycle around AI agents. Our engineers don't just write code. They orchestrate fleets of AI agents that plan, build, and verify production software in parallel. Sometimes, almost no code is written by humans — only audited.

**What we are:** A product engineering studio that has rebuilt everything around AI agents.
**What we are not:** A generic outsourcing agency, a staffing company, a body shop, or a consulting firm.

### Core Values

- **Integrity** — Honest communication. We say what's real, not what sounds good.
- **Agility** — We adapt fast. To client feedback, to new tools, to market shifts.
- **Empathy** — We understand the people we work with. Clients and teammates.
- **Ownership** — We own outcomes, not just tasks. Proactive, accountable, committed.

## Voice Principles

### The One-Line Version

SoluteLabs sounds like a sharp technical co-founder explaining how they actually build things — informed, direct, practical, and genuinely excited about the work without being salesy about it.

### Intelligent but not jargon-heavy

We know our stuff — Claude Code internals, RAG architectures, multi-agent orchestration, spec-driven dev. We use technical terms when they're the right terms. But we always explain. We never use jargon to sound smart. We use it because it's precise.

*Do:* "We use CLAUDE.md files — project constitutions that define architecture constraints, coding standards, and business context — committed to git and persistent across the entire project lifecycle."
*Don't:* "We leverage cutting-edge AI paradigms to synergistically optimize development workflows."

### Confident but not arrogant

We've shipped 150+ products since 2014. We're good at this. We say so directly, backed by evidence. But we never punch down at competitors, clients' internal teams, or the industry. We're confident in our approach — not dismissive of others'.

*Do:* "We operate at Gen 3 of AI-assisted engineering. Here's what that means and why it matters."
*Don't:* "While other agencies are still in the stone age, we've already..."

### Practical but not boring

We ground everything in real implementation, real case studies, real trade-offs. We don't do hand-wavy thought leadership. But practical doesn't mean dry — we have opinions, we tell stories, and we're not afraid to say what we think.

*Do:* "We built 3 RAG systems last quarter. Every single one needed a different architecture. Here's how we decide."
*Don't:* "RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is a methodology that combines..."

### Collaborative but not subservient

We position as partners, not vendors. We work alongside our clients' teams, not below them. We push back when something is wrong. We bring product thinking, not just code. But we never talk down to clients or act like we know their business better than they do.

*Do:* "We'll work with your engineering team to define the spec, then our agents execute in parallel."
*Don't:* "Just tell us what to build and we'll get it done."

### Forward-looking but not hype-driven

We're genuinely excited about AI transforming how software gets built. We document our journey openly — including the mistakes. But we never hype things we haven't done or claim outcomes we can't prove.

*Do:* "80%+ of our production code is AI-generated. But that stat means nothing without context. Here's the real breakdown..."
*Don't:* "AI will replace all software engineers by 2027. Here's how we're leading the revolution."

### The "I Am / But I Am Not" Framework

Use this to calibrate voice quickly:

| I am... | But I am not... |
|---------|----------------|
| Intelligent | Using excessive jargon |
| Friendly and casual | Without merit or substance |
| Opinionated | Arrogant or dismissive |
| Conversational | Disorganized or rambling |
| Confident | Boastful or exaggerating |
| Technical | Inaccessible to non-engineers |
| Excited about AI | Hype-driven or breathless |
| Direct | Blunt or cold |
| Educational | Patronizing or condescending |

## Tone by Channel

The voice stays consistent. The tone flexes by context.

### Website (Homepage, Service Pages, Case Studies)
**Tone:** Authoritative, outcome-driven, technically credible
- Lead with outcomes and proof points, not descriptions
- Use specific numbers, timelines, and named tools
- Write for the CTO who has 30 seconds to decide if you're worth a call
- Every page should answer: "What can they do, how fast, and can they prove it?"

### Blog Posts
**Tone:** Insightful, educational, opinionated
- Write like an experienced engineer sharing knowledge with peers
- Take positions. "Here's how we decide" is better than "Here are some options"
- Ground everything in real projects, real tools, real trade-offs
- First paragraph should be a concise summary of the post's core argument (helps LLM indexing and reader scanning)
- Target length: 800–2,500 words. Quality over length, always.
- Minimum 3–4 internal links to other SoluteLabs blog posts or service pages
- Include relevant visuals, diagrams, or code snippets — minimum 2 per post

### Social Media (Company Page)
**Tone:** Informative, sharp, occasionally playful
- Short-form. Get to the point in the first line.
- Share insights, not announcements. "We learned X" beats "We're thrilled to announce Y."
- Use frameworks, numbered lists, and quick takes
- Never post generic AI news without a specific SoluteLabs angle

### Email (Outbound, Cadences)
**Tone:** Personal, direct, respectful of attention
- Write like Karan or a senior team member, not like a marketing automation tool
- Reference something specific about the recipient's company
- Short. 4–6 sentences max for cold outreach.
- CTA should be low-friction: "Worth a conversation?" not "Schedule a 30-minute demo"

### Proposals & Sales Docs
**Tone:** Precise, confident, solutions-oriented
- Lead with understanding of the client's problem
- Show the approach before the price
- Use case study proof points throughout
- Never use filler phrases ("We would be delighted to..." — just state what you'll do)

## Language Rules

### Words and Phrases to Avoid

These words signal generic corporate writing. Replace them with clearer alternatives.

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|-------------|
| Navigate | Manage, handle, address, tackle |
| Leverage | Use, take advantage of, apply |
| Synergy | Collaboration, teamwork |
| Paradigm shift | Fundamental change, major shift |
| Seamless | Smooth, effortless |
| Bandwidth | Capacity, ability, resources |
| Win-win | Mutually beneficial |
| Cutting-edge | Advanced, innovative, state-of-the-art |
| Empower | Enable, equip, support |
| Holistic approach | Comprehensive approach |
| Best-in-class | Top-tier, exceptional, leading |
| Unlock potential | Realize opportunities, harness potential |
| Game-changing | Transformative, significant |
| Elevate | Improve, enhance, boost |
| End-to-end solution | Complete solution, full-stack solution |
| Thought leadership | (Just demonstrate expertise — don't label it) |
| We're thrilled to announce | (Just say the thing) |
| Disrupt | Transform, change, reshape |
| Value proposition | What sets us apart, core benefit |
| Move the needle | Make a measurable difference, improve results |
| Circle back | Follow up, revisit |
| Low-hanging fruit | Quick wins, immediate opportunities |
| Deep dive | Detailed look, closer examination |
| At the end of the day | Ultimately, in practice |
| Ecosystem | (Use only when technically accurate, e.g., "plugin ecosystem") |

### Phrases We DO Use

These are part of the SoluteLabs vocabulary. Use them naturally, not forcefully:

- Spec-driven development
- Multi-agent orchestration
- Production-grade / production-ready
- CLAUDE.md / project constitutions
- Agent skills, subagents, MCP
- Gen 3 engineering
- The harness (when referring to our agentic stack)
- "Ship" (not "deliver" or "deploy" in marketing contexts)
- "Orchestrate" (what our engineers do with AI agents)
- "Audit" (what humans do with AI-generated code)

### Writing Style Rules

**Active voice always.** "We built an AI agent system" not "An AI agent system was built by our team."

**Short paragraphs.** 2–4 sentences max. One idea per paragraph. If you're writing a wall of text, break it up.

**Specifics over generics.** "22,000 lines of production code in under a week" not "we build things fast." Name the tools. Name the frameworks. Name the timelines.

**Show, don't claim.** Instead of "we're experts in AI," describe a specific project where you solved a hard AI problem. The reader infers the expertise.

**Second person for the reader.** Use "you" and "your" to speak directly to the reader. "Your engineering team" not "engineering teams."

**No exclamation marks.** One exception: Karan's social posts, sparingly, where enthusiasm is genuine.

**No emoji in formal content.** Website, proposals, blogs — no emoji. Social media — emoji is fine, used sparingly and purposefully.

**Numbers under 10: spell out.** Numbers 10 and above: use digits. Exception: stats and data points always use digits ("4.7★ Clutch", "150+ products", "80%+").

### Visual Content Rules

- Minimum 2 images, diagrams, or code snippets per blog post
- Prefer diagrams, architecture visuals, and screenshots over stock photos
- If using illustrations, maintain a consistent style (not random Unsplash photos)
- Infographics welcome when they genuinely clarify complex topics

### AI-Generated Content Policy

SoluteLabs is an AI-native company. We use AI tools extensively in content creation — the same way we use them in engineering. This is a strength, not something to hide.

**The rule:** Content must feel authentic, informed, and distinctly SoluteLabs — regardless of whether a human, an LLM, or a combination wrote it. The test is not "what percentage was AI-generated" but "would a CTO reading this feel like they're hearing from experienced engineers who actually build this stuff?"

**In practice:**
- AI tools can and should assist with research, drafts, structure, and ideation
- Every piece of content must be reviewed and refined by someone who understands the subject matter
- If using an LLM to generate content, always ground it with specific SoluteLabs examples, real project details, and opinions that come from actual experience
- The voice must match this guide. LLM-default voice (you know the one — formal, hedging, "it's important to note that...") is not SoluteLabs voice
- Karan's social posts require his personal review and voice editing before publishing, regardless of who or what drafted them

## What SoluteLabs Never Does

These are absolute rules. No exceptions, regardless of channel or context.

1. **Never trash competitors or clients' internal teams.** We position our approach as an evolution, not as a critique of anyone else's.
2. **Never make claims we can't back with evidence.** If we say "5–10x faster," we better have the case study. If we don't, say "significantly faster" and add the context.
3. **Never use "we're thrilled/excited/delighted to announce."** Just say the thing.
4. **Never write walls of unstructured text.** Use subheadings, short paragraphs, and visual breaks.
5. **Never publish Karan's social content without his voice review.** Ghostwriting is fine. Publishing without his approval is not.
6. **Never hide our India base.** We're headquartered in Ahmedabad. We have a US entity. We serve global clients. This is stated factually, never apologized for.
7. **Never position as a staffing/body shop.** We're a product engineering studio, not a talent marketplace.
8. **Never lead with technology when talking to non-technical buyers.** Lead with outcomes. The technology is the "how," not the "what."
9. **Never overplay a single metric.** The 22K LOC stat is a proof point in a case study, not the company headline. The 80%+ stat needs context every time.
10. **Never use headcount as a selling point.** "25 people" is not impressive. "25 people delivering like 75" is — but frame it as output, not size.
